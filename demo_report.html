What this number means

73.5% of your text corpus lives in clusters that are well-separated from their nearest neighbours, as judged by the LLM under your equivalence rule.

A cluster scores high when an item from a neighbouring cluster, secretly planted among its members, is reliably spotted and isolated by the LLM. A low score means the cluster blurs into its neighbours — the LLM cannot tell them apart under your rule.

Weighting is by cluster size, so a large failing cluster pulls the score down more than a small one.

How it is computed

Step 1 — Calibration. The LLM is run on two kinds of planted controls: (a) pure draws — k items from the same cluster — to measure γ, the rate the LLM accidentally isolates a truly-same item; (b) far draws — k−1 home items plus one from a distant cluster — to verify the judge can detect obvious intruders (gate threshold: ≥ 70%).

Step 2 — Intruder detection. For each cluster, one item from a near-neighbour cluster is planted among k=? home items and the LLM sorts them by kind. Detected = planted item is a singleton group.

Step 3 — Correction. Raw detection rates are corrected for chance isolation: corrected = (raw − γ) / (1 − γ).

Step 4 — Aggregation. Each cluster's corrected rate is thresholded at 50%. The headline is the size-weighted fraction above that threshold.

How much to trust it

Calibration gate: PASS. PASS = the judge reliably detects obvious intruders, so the correction is meaningful. FAIL = the judge or the rule is too weak; fix before reading scores.

Per-cluster 95% confidence intervals (lo–hi column) reflect how many intruder draws were made per cluster (n). Narrow the intervals by raising n_draws or coverage_target.
